The Challenge

Making NASA Technology Accessible to Farmers

Climate change is creating unprecedented challenges for farmers‚Äîunpredictable weather patterns, shifting growing seasons, and increased extreme weather events. While NASA has powerful satellite technology that could help, it's largely inaccessible to the people who need it most.

The challenge was to:

  • Make complex NASA satellite data understandable and actionable for farmers
  • Provide hyper-local insights (field-level, not just regional)
  • Deliver recommendations that fit into existing farming workflows
  • Build trust with a user base that may be skeptical of technology
  • Design for users who may have limited tech experience or connectivity
  • Complete a working prototype in 48 hours for the hackathon

The Approach

Insights Over Data

Rather than overwhelming farmers with raw satellite imagery and weather data, we focused on providing clear, actionable insights. The key was understanding how farmers think about their land and what decisions they need to make.

NASA Technologies

  • HSEG (Hierarchical Segmentation Engine) - Provides pixel-level farmland segmentation into distinct soil and crop zones, allowing farmers to see variations across their fields
  • MERRA/AS & Climate Analytics as a Service - Delivers weather pattern forecasting and climate trend analysis for long-term planning

Design Principles

  • Zone-based thinking - Match how farmers mentally divide their land
  • Action-oriented - Every insight leads to a clear next step
  • Visual-first - Maps and charts over tables and numbers
  • Progressive disclosure - Summary first, details on demand
  • Offline-capable - Work without constant connectivity

The Discovery

Understanding Farmer Workflows

Through research and conversations with farming stakeholders, we identified key decision points where C.L.I.M.A could provide value:

  • Irrigation planning - When and where to water based on soil moisture and weather forecasts
  • Fertilizer application - Which zones need nutrients and how much
  • Planting strategy - Optimal timing and crop selection based on climate trends
  • Harvest planning - Predicting yield and timing based on growth patterns
  • Climate adaptation - Long-term planning for shifting weather patterns

Detailed Design

Dashboard Layout

The dashboard prioritizes actionable insights over raw data. At a glance, farmers see:

  • Current field conditions with zone-by-zone breakdown
  • Immediate action items (e.g., "Zone 3 needs irrigation within 48 hours")
  • 7-day weather forecast with impact on field operations
  • Long-term climate trends affecting planting decisions

Zone-Based Interface

Using HSEG segmentation data, the interface divides farmland into distinct zones based on soil composition, moisture levels, and crop health. This matches how farmers naturally think about their land‚Äînot as a uniform field, but as areas with different characteristics and needs.

Recommendation System

Rather than just showing data, C.L.I.M.A provides specific recommendations:

  • "Zone 2 shows low nitrogen levels - apply 40 lbs/acre"
  • "Forecast shows rain in 3 days - delay irrigation in Zones 1 and 4"
  • "Climate trends suggest shifting corn planting 2 weeks earlier next season"

Visual Data Representation

All data is presented visually through maps, heat maps, and simple charts. Color coding makes it easy to spot problem areas at a glance. Detailed numbers are available on tap for those who want them.

The Framework

Technical Architecture

We built an MVP React web app that demonstrates the integration of NASA technologies:

  • NASA HSEG API integration for field segmentation
  • NASA MERRA/AS API for climate and weather data
  • Data processing pipeline to convert satellite imagery into actionable insights
  • React frontend with responsive design for mobile and desktop
  • Map visualization using satellite imagery overlays

Lessons Learned

This project taught me the importance of designing for domain experts who aren't tech experts. Farmers are incredibly knowledgeable about their craft, but may not be familiar with satellite imagery or climate analytics. The key was respecting their expertise while making technology accessible‚Äîproviding insights, not just data, and matching their mental models of how they think about their land.
